1

The Ripple

Large-scale mural · Coquitlam City Centre · Raven-Tacuara Artist Collective
About the artists

The Raven-Tacuara Artist Collective comprises Indigenous artists Amanda Hugon, Stephanie Anderson, and Facundo Gastiazoro. Specializing in large-scale public murals, the collective integrates traditional Indigenous storytelling with contemporary street art techniques, emphasizing cultural heritage, environmental stewardship, and community collaboration. For The Ripple, the collective partnered with the kʷikʷəƛ̓əm First Nation and received a formal blessing to ensure the work accurately reflects local Coast Salish protocols and historical narratives.

Visual description and analysis

Anchored by a central design inspired by a traditional Coast Salish spindle whorl, The Ripple sends concentric visual waves outward, carrying vibrant, stylized depictions of local flora and fauna. This radial composition powerfully illustrates the interconnectedness of all living things within the Coquitlam watershed.

The spindle whorl — historically used to spin wool — here metaphorically spins the continuous threads of life and community. Bold colors and sharp geometric forms create a distinct sense of movement, representing the ongoing cycle of life and the resilience of Indigenous cultures.

By placing prominent Indigenous iconography in a busy urban centre, the collective actively reclaims public space. The mural functions not merely as aesthetic decoration, but as a visual declaration of territorial stewardship — disrupting the colonial urban landscape and reminding a diverse public of the deep and enduring Indigenous presence on these lands.

Access & Navigation

Accessible via the Millennium Line SkyTrain (Lafarge Lake-Douglas Station). The site is flat and fully wheelchair accessible. No admission required. Open year-round.

2

Coquitlam 125 Salmon Project

12 fiberglass sculptures · City-wide · Jody Broomfield (Sinámkin)
About the artist

Jody Broomfield (Sinámkin) is a Coast Salish artist from the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh (Squamish) Nation. A full-time artist since 1999, he apprenticed under master carver Klatle Bhi. Broomfield is renowned for his mastery of traditional Coast Salish design elements across diverse mediums including carved wood, etched glass, and large-scale public metal and fiberglass sculpture. His public works — encompassing welcome figures and prominent cultural markers — are dedicated to educating the broader public on the richness of Squamish and Coast Salish heritage.

Visual description and analysis

The project consists of twelve identical, six-foot-tall fiberglass salmon sculptures distributed across the city. Broomfield's base form is characterized by smooth, streamlined contours that capture the forward motion of a salmon swimming upstream.

Carved into the surface are quintessential Coast Salish aesthetic elements — ovoids, U-forms, and split U-forms — that break down the salmon's anatomy into abstract, fluid geometries representing both spiritual and physical vitality.

The subject matter directly references the name "Coquitlam," making the work a literal embodiment of the land's history. The repetition of these massive sculptures across twelve distinct city locations functions as a large-scale territorial marker: as viewers navigate from one to the next, the art physically maps historical migratory routes, reminding a multicultural public of the historical and ongoing importance of salmon to the sustenance and spiritual life of local Coast Salish peoples.

Access & Navigation

A city-wide scavenger hunt across 12 locations. Recommended starting points: Blue Mountain Park (975 King Albert Ave) and Town Centre Park. Drive or cycle between mapped pins. Free parking available at Blue Mountain Park. Paved walking paths throughout.

About this walk

This public art walk takes place on the unceded traditional territory of the kʷikʷəƛ̓əm (Kwikwetlem) First Nation. We acknowledge their stewardship of these lands and waters since time immemorial, and encourage all visitors to learn more about local Indigenous history and the ongoing work of reconciliation.

The artworks presented on this walk were chosen for their capacity to communicate Coast Salish cultural heritage to a wide and multicultural audience. Both works are rooted in community consultation and reflect the living, evolving nature of Indigenous artistic traditions — not relics of the past, but active interventions in the present.

kʷikʷəƛ̓əm (Kwikwetlem) First Nation

The Kwikwetlem people have lived on these lands since time immemorial. Their name — and the name "Coquitlam" itself — is derived from the word meaning "red fish up the river," honouring the sockeye salmon that sustained their communities and spiritual life. The Kwikwetlem First Nation continues to assert sovereignty over their traditional territory and to lead cultural revitalization efforts in the region.
